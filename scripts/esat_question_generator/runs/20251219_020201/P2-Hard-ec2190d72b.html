QUESTION
A cylindrical resistor of fixed volume and length$L$dissipates power$P$when a potential difference$V$is applied across its ends. The resistor is reshaped to a length of$2L$. What is the power dissipated in the reshaped resistor when the same potential difference$V$is applied?
OPTIONS
A: $4P$
B: $2P$
C: $P$
D: $\frac{P}{2}$
E: $\frac{P}{4}$ âœ“ CORRECT

Correct Answer: E

SOLUTION
Reasoning:

The resistance of the wire is given by$R = \frac{\rho L}{A}$. Since the volume of the resistor is fixed, we have$\text{Volume} = A \times L = \text{constant}$. Rearranging for area gives$A = \frac{\text{Volume}}{L}$. Substituting this into the resistance formula:$$R = \frac{\rho L}{(\text{Volume}/L)} = \frac{\rho L^2}{\text{Volume}}$$Thus, resistance is proportional to the square of the length ($R \propto L^2$). When the length doubles ($L \to 2L$), the resistance increases by a factor of$2^2 = 4$. So the new resistance is$4R$. The power dissipated is given by$P = \frac{V^2}{R}$. Since the potential difference$V$is constant, power is inversely proportional to resistance ($P \propto \frac{1}{R}$). Therefore, the new power is:$$P' = \frac{V^2}{4R} = \frac{1}{4} \left( \frac{V^2}{R} \right) = \frac{P}{4}$$Hence the correct answer is$\frac{P}{4}$.
Key Insight:

For a wire of fixed volume, resistance scales with$L^2$, not$L$.
