What is the value of the sum $$ \sum_{n=1}^{99} \frac{1}{\sqrt{n} + \sqrt{n+1}} $$


OPTIONS:
  A: \sqrt{99} - 1
  B: 9 [CORRECT]
  C: \sqrt{99}
  D: 10
  E: 11
CORRECT_OPTION: B

SOLUTION:
REASONING:
Rationalise the denominator of the general term: $$ \frac{1}{\sqrt{n} + \sqrt{n+1}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{n} + \sqrt{n+1}} \times \frac{\sqrt{n+1} - \sqrt{n}}{\sqrt{n+1} - \sqrt{n}} = \frac{\sqrt{n+1} - \sqrt{n}}{(n+1) - n} = \sqrt{n+1} - \sqrt{n} $$ The sum becomes a telescoping series: $$ \sum_{n=1}^{99} (\sqrt{n+1} - \sqrt{n}) = (\sqrt{2} - \sqrt{1}) + (\sqrt{3} - \sqrt{2}) + \dots + (\sqrt{100} - \sqrt{99}) $$ All intermediate terms cancel, leaving only the final positive term and the initial negative term: $$ \sqrt{100} - \sqrt{1} = 10 - 1 = 9 $$


KEY_INSIGHT:
Rationalising the denominator transforms the summand into a difference, creating a telescoping series where only the boundary terms survive.
